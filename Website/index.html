Important LGBT+ Terms

What does the acronym stand for?

LGBT stands for Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, and Transgender. The + at the end is a shortening of the full acronym which has different meanings based on who you ask. In general, the full spectrum expands to include more sexualities, such as Aromantic/Asexual people, and also more gender identities, such as the Indigenous American Two-Spirit identity. You can read more about the full acronym, and various other LGBT+ terms, here.

The History of the acronym

The LGBT+ acronym has its origins in what was originally called the gay rights movement, or sometimes the gay and lesbian rights movement. Starting in the 1980s this grew to include bisexual people, marking the first version of the acronym as "LGB". Later, especially beginning in the 1990s as Transgender people became more accepted within the community, the acronym was expanded to "LGBT". The addition of more letters has been a recent development, and has been spurred on by the internet and the flourishing of individual LGBT activism, and so the "+" at the end indicates the many different versions of the acronym, such as "LGBTQIA", "LGBTQQ", "LGBTQ2S", etc. In general, today LGBT+ refers to anybody who exists outside of the heteronormative lifestyle.

What is heteronormativity?

Heteronormativity is the societal idea that being straight and cisgendered is the normal and "default" way of existing. Anything other than being straight and cis in a heteronormative society is considered, if not outright evil and threatening, strange and to be avoided. Heteronormativity is important to understand as a deeply ingrained public sensibility that presents itself in many ways. Heteronormativity is not just explicit homophobia, but also implicit, subconscious biases - for example, somebody who follows a heteronormative viewpoint may feel surprised and betrayed if their favorite TV show character is revealed to be gay - even if they support LGBT+ rights on a political level. Somebody doesn't necessarily have to be outright and proudly bigoted to hold a subconscious view that certain sexualities are "normal" and others are "strange".

History: The First Pride Parade

Gay rights before stonewall

Before 1969, the gay rights movement in the USA existed mainly as a smaller section of the larger Civil Rights movement and sexual revolution. The objective of gay rights activism was less focused on changing American culture to include gay people, and more on showing off that gay people could fit in to American culture. A good example of this is the annual reminder - a photo of which is included here - which was an annual march held in front of a government building during the 1960s. Note that the signs here use the word homosexual, rather than gay, the presence of the American flag, and how formally dressed they all are. In fact, the annual reminder had a very strict dress code. The goal of the Annual Reminder was to present gay people as "just as normal as the rest of American society".

The Stonewall Riot

The Stonewall Inn was - and still is - a gay bar in New York City. During the 1960s, the NYC government refused to give out liquor licenses to bars known to serve gay people so that police would have an excuse to harass them. This occurred in Stonewall, but in 1969 patrons of the bar resisted arrest, protested, and eventually as word spread through the NYC gay community, the incident evolved into multiple days of rioting.

Inspiration

The Stonewall Riots were not conducted by the same protestors who organized the Annual Reminder. The Riots were conducted by drunk gay men attending a bar, by drag queens, people from all parts of the LGBT+ spectrum, and importantly, lower-class city folk rather than the usual white, suburban middle class protestors who were more common at the Annual Reminder. This inspired various different organizations fighting for gay rights, such as the Gay Liberation Front, to change their tactics. The idea would no longer be about placing gay people inside of American culture, but about expanding American culture to include gay people, regardless of their appearance, status, etc. There would be no more strict dress codes, nor usage of an old scientific term with negative connotations - homosexual - but instead a new and exciting word: gay. In 1970, on the 1-year anniversary of the Stonewall riot, pride parades were held in New York, LA, and other cities.

These pride parades were celebratory in tone. The idea was to create a fun and inclusive environment to influence American culture, rather than staging a traditional protest march that would risk state violence. This was especially true of the march in LA, where the request for a permit to protest included the possibility that an elephant would march through the streets. By doing this, the Pride marchers aimed to invite bystanders to join in on the celebrations and see that gay and lesbian people were not strange or worrisome, but that being gay or lesbian could in fact be freeing and fun. By doing this, they hoped, they could influence American culture by showing that opening society to openly gay people would benefit society at large.

The Gay Rights Movement in 1970

In her book Pride Parades, Katherine Bruce writes that the purpose of Pride in the 1970s was to proclaim the "cultural worth of gays and lesbians". Notably absent from this message are bisexuals and transgender people - this despite the fact that many transgender people participated in the Stonewall Riot. It is worth noting, again, that the T in LGBT was not added until the 1980s. The gay rights movement was also headed almost entirely by white gay men, which of course affected the main focus of the group. While women and people of color were never explicitly excluded from the gay rights movement, white gay men were the largest demographic in the leadership and membership of the movement. This is the main reason this section refers to the movement as the "gay rights movement" rather than the "LGBT+" movement - it simply was not fully LGBT inclusive at this point in time.

While pride parades were certainly affecting American culture and the visibility of gay people, legal progress was extremely slow. Gay marriage would not become legal anywhere in the USA until Massachusetts became the first state to legalize it in 2004. The LA Pride Parade in 1970 almost didn't happen - or would have happened in a much different character - as it lacked institutional support. The police chief of LA attempted to prevent the protest from getting permission to occur, his logic being that gay people were inherently morally compromised - "We would be ill-advised to discommode the people to have a burglars' or robbers' parade or a homosexuals' parade." It would take a lot of slow progress to change culture enough to affect the legal standing of LGBT people.

The Expanding Movement: Transgender Rights

Through the 1970s and for some time during the 1980s, transgender people - and bisexual people - were largely considered to be outside the purview of the gay rights movement. This led to two different phenomena: transgender activists either pushing for further inclusion of transgender people within the pride movement, or deciding to organize their own separate protest marches. Transgender specific protests are still held today, and are sometimes associated with pride organizations, and sometimes held and organized entirely separately. This is, in some ways, a result of the inherent difference between advocacy for the rights associated with someone's sexual preferences and someone's gender identity. While the legalization of gay marriage in 2015 was a huge victory for lesbians, gays, and bisexuals, the same cannot be said for transgender people.

Today, transgender people are generally accepted as a celebrated and necessary part of the LGBT+ community. That said, the inclusion of trans people in the pride movement is still a developing phenomenon. The acronym of LGBT did not enter popular use until the '90s. Reactionary groups within the pride movement, such as the LGB alliance, still seek to exclude transgender people from pride. Katherine Bruce, who interviewed many trans people at Pride events for her book, noted a common sentiment that "lesbians, gays, and bisexuals are willing to 'throw [trans people] under the bus' to get their own legal protections." In general, however, the LGBT+ movement as a whole is beginning to shift its focus towards trans rights. This is especially true due to transgender people's rights currently being under much heavier national scrutiny than other sections of the LGBT+ spectrum.

Katherine Bruce notes that the reason for the inclusion of transgender people in the LGBT rights movement is due to the overlap of sexuality and gender in most western cultures. In traditional American culture, the idea of "being a man" is inherently tied to the idea of being heterosexual - men should marry women and have children to form a nuclear family - and therefore simply by existing gay and bisexual men call into question American gender roles. The idea applies similarly for lesbian and bisexual women as well. Transgender people do this much more explicitly by openly identifying as a gender unrelated to their sex. Because of this, any cultural force or legal legislation that reinforces heteronormative sexual and gender roles is certain to harm all members of the LGBT+ community, regardless of their specific sexuality or gender identity.

Challenges Facing The LGBT+ Movement Today

In 2015 gay marriage was legalized across the USA via supreme court decision. This was a massive victory for the LGBT+ movement - but it is not the end of the road. There are still many states that lack discrimination protection in the job market for openly lesbian, gay, or bisexual people. Additionally, transgender rights are under attack in many states across the USA today - such as the multitude of anti-trans bills passed by Florida governor Ron Destantis. This follows a sort of 'last-hired-first-fired' trend for reactionary backlash to LGBT+ rights - the battle against gay marriage is seen as 'lost', and so now the focus shifts to the group that still has not obtained their full rights - transgender people. Note that this is a reactionary backlash, not solely a traditional Conservative one - some nominally progressive groups like the LGB alliance and radical feminists have also rallied around their concerns about transgender rights. Because of this, the LGBT+ movement must also shift its focus to supporting transgender rights - as explained before, attacks on trans rights will inevitably also turn into attacks on LGBT+ rights in general.

Institutional vs Cultural Support

In addition to the legal recognition of gay marriage, Pride has been successful in ingraining its message within American culture and politics. Today, many political institutions in the US are openly supportive of Pride events - for example, the University of Utah sponsored their own Pride parade in 2021. In the south, the University of Tennessee simply had an article on their website directing people to other websites that discussed LGBT+ history. Current president Joe Biden has tweeted support for the LGBT+ movement and hung pride flags from the white house. It is debatable how much of this is real support for LGBT+ rights - as nice as website articles and tweets are, they are not actual institutional change. That being said, if nothing else all of these are signs that LGBT+ rights are now popular enough to be advertised politicians hoping for re-election.

Commercialization

As pride has grown more popular, it has received institutional support not just from the government but also from corporations. This is an issue that is more controversial within and without the Pride movement. It a good thing to show off being openly gay while protesting for the rights of yourself and others - but being sponsored by Pepsi while doing so may muddy the message. This phenomenon can also be a distraction from 'actual' political issues that are much more important - instead of having a candid debate about the place of transgender people in American society, it is easy for politicians and pundits to change the subject and simply complain about how Pride has become corporate - "how could these people be oppressed if so many companies have rainbow pictures on their twitters for a month?".

Shifting Focus

Today, the fight for gay marriage having been won, lesbians, gays, and bisexuals stand at a fairly decent spot within American society. This is not to say everything is perfect - in fact, since the legal argument that got gay marriage legalized relied on precedent from Roe v. Wade, now overturned, gay marriage could even be at risk in the near future. For the moment, though, the focus of the pride movement is generally shifting onto the rights of other communities within the movement. An interviewee at Chicago Pride 2022 put it candidly: "Pride right now is not about white men anymore. It's about our marginalized communities within the gay community." This especially includes those whose rights are currently under national scrutiny, such as transgender people, and those who do not benefit from race and class privileges, such as LGBT+ people of color.

This shift is not just a move to fight for people's rights. It is also a somewhat necessary pivot to remain politically relevant. Ever since the victory of gay marriage in 2015, the American public largely considers the fight for gay rights as "won" - whether or not that's actually true. To remain politically relevant the Pride movement therefore needs to find new grounds to fight on. This does not make the fight for the rights of transgender people and other minorities less important or less genuine, but simply that the pivot towards other marginalized groups within the LGBT+ spectrum is both necessary and strategic.

The Future of Pride

Pride's Strengths

One of pride's main strengths, and something it should continue to focus on, has been its ability to influence popular culture. By hosting parades, instead of protests, pride invites everyone - including bystanders and allies - to participate. People who would normally not show up to march at a protest, not being willing to risk their safety, can easily attend a pride parade to support the community, and even have a fun time. This invites the rest of culture to not just tolerate LGBT+ people, but to enjoy their presence. This, together with more traditional activism, has created a wave of celebration of LGBT+ people and their identities, importantly and increasingly so in mainstream American media, compounding the effect of the movement.

Another very important rhetorical strength of the LGBT+ movement which should continue to be emphasized is its intersectionality. This was created in the past by expanding the umbrella of the movement to also include bisexual and transgender people after the gay rights movement gained traction. In addition to this, the pride movement embraces the struggles of many other groups, such as black rights and womenâ€™s rights activism. This is possible because many of the rights that LGBT+ people fight for are not exclusive to LGBT+ people, but applicable to progressive movements in general. For example, the argument that won Roe v. Wade and the right to abortion in the USA, that the people have a right to privacy and bodily autonomy from the government, was the same argument that was used to argue for the right to gay marriage. The rights that transgender people fight for - those of bodily autonomy, the right to self-affirming care, redefining what constitutes medically necessary care, access to things like birth control and hormonal regulators, etc. - are all struggles that trans people share in common with feminism. This means that the LGBT+ movement is strengthened by, and strengthens, the success of many other movements.

Pride's Weaknesses

By focusing so much on changing cultural attitudes, Pride has been extremely slow to obtain legal recognition of its goals. For a comparison, we can consider the trajectory of the Civil Rights Movement. While much black activism existed before it, for the sake of argument we can consider the Montgomery Bus Boycott, which began in 1955, as a starting point for the Civil Rights Movement of that era. From the boycott to the passing of the Civil Rights Act, when the movement gained federal and legal recognition, was a period of 9 years. Similarly, while much activism existed before it, we can consider the Stonewall Riot to be the inciting incident of the LGBT+ Pride movement. It took 46 years - from 1969 to 2015 - for the Pride movement to go from the Stonewall Riot to federal legalization of gay marriage. The pride movement could stand to focus more on actual political and legal gains, and perhaps expand its activities to more than simple parades - pride organizations should also focus on direct action, boycotts, marches, and other forms of protest throughout the year, rather than simply on parades every June.

Conclusion

With the legalization of gay marriage, the passing of many protections for LGBT+ people on state levels, and increasing support from politicians, the LGBT+ Pride movement has made huge strides since its beginnings in the 1970s. There is still a long way to go, especially for the rights of transgender people. The task now is to retain Pride's political momentum - rather than just its cultural momentum - to ensure that Pride can continue to improve LGBT+ rights. To do this, it must embrace its intersectionality and also focus on more explicit political activism.
