1914 Beginning of World War One On one side the Entente: Britain, France, and Russia. On the other side the central powers: Germany and Austria-Hungary.
1915 Italian entry into World War One The Italians resisted joining the war, largely due to a large and organized anti-war movement in the country. Italy joined the Entente after being promised land gains in the Balkans.
1916 Growth of the anti-war and interventionist movements Those who advocated for Italy to continue fighting in World War One were called interventionists. Intervention was generally justified either on Liberal grounds, viewing the war as a crusade of Demoracy, or on nationalist grounds, viewing the war as an opportunity to expand Italy's borders.
1917 Italian defeat in the Battle of Caporetto In October of 1917 the Italian army suffered a horrendous defeat to the Austrian army in the Battle of Caporetto. The battle caused political radicalism in Italian politics, with pacifists generally allying with the Socialist or Catholic left and interventionists allying with the nationalist Right.
1918 End of World War One
1919 Signing of the Treaty of Versailles

Gatherings of Fasci

Italian elections		 The Treaty of Versailles was Signed in June 1919, and granted Italy minor territorial gains in former Austrian lands. Many of the nationalist-interventionists considered the Treaty of Versailles a betrayal, specifically angry that Italy was not granted Dalmatia in the Balkans.

Mussolini entered Italian politics by organizing gatherings of interventionists he called the Fasci.

The first elections in Italy to feature universal male suffrage were held in 1919. The two parties that performed best were the Italian Socialist Party and the left-wing-Catholic Italian Popular Party.
1920 The Red Biennial

Occupation of Fiume		 Immediately after the end of the Great War the left wing grew rapidly. From 1918 to 1920 membership in the Socialist Party's labor union grew from 250,000 members to 2.2 million. The growth allowed Socialists to push for radical reforms in government.

From 1919 to 1920 about 2,500 Italian nationalists invaded and occupied the town of Fiume, on the coast of Dalmatia, in protest of the Treaty of Versailles not giving Italy more territory. It inspired Fasci in Italy to become more violent.
1921 Socialists Split

Right-wing backlash

National Fascist Party officially founded by Mussolini		 The Socialist party, despite massive success in the 1919 elections, was unable to maintain control over the government because of the split between reformist socialism and revolutionary communism. In 1921 the Communists formerly left the Socialist party.

Beginning in 1921 Mussolini's Fasci began violently attacking Socialist and Communist strongholds. These attacks successfully held local governments hostage and forced the resignation of left-leaning politians. The Italian police rarely did anything to stop it.
1922 Fascist Coup Throughout 1922 the National Fascist Party (PNF) used their "militant branch" (blackshirts) to violently take over Italian local governments. This violence culminated in October 1922 with the March on Rome, when 50,000 Italian Fascists demanded that Mussolini be made Prime Minister of Italy. The King of Italy named Mussolini Prime Minister.
1923 Fascist power consolidation The PNF formed a coalition government in the Italian parliament together with nationalists, authoritarian Liberals, and Conservative Catholics. To buy the support of industrialists and landlords, the Fascists weakened labor unions. To buy the support of the Catholic Church, the Fascists mandated Catholicism in education.
1924 Complete Fascist victory After the 1924 elections were obviously tampered with by the Facsist party, Socialists and Liberals left parliament in opposition. In response, the Fascists threatened violence against anybody who spoke out, and kidnapped and murdered a Socialist Member of parliament. The rebellion against Fascism failed, and the Fascist party reigned supreme.
